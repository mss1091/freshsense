The Science

How Food Talks to Our Sensors

Every food item emits a unique cocktail of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) as it decays. FreshSense listens to this chemical conversation.

🧬 What Are VOCs?

Volatile Organic Compounds are airborne molecules released during the natural metabolic processes of food degradation. As bacteria break down proteins, carbohydrates, and fats, they release signature gases that are invisible to the human eye but measurable by electronic sensors — long before you can smell anything off.

KEY SPOILAGE MARKERS

Ethylene (C₂H₄)Fruit ripening & over-ripening
Ammonia (NH₃)Protein/amino acid breakdown
Hydrogen Sulfide (H₂S)Egg & meat decomposition
Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)General metabolic decay

📡 MOS Sensor Technology

Metal-Oxide Semiconductor (MOS) gas sensors work by measuring changes in electrical resistance when target gas molecules adsorb onto a heated metal-oxide surface. Different MOS materials exhibit selective sensitivity to different gas families, enabling our multi-sensor array to create a distinct chemical "fingerprint" for each decay stage.

WHY THIS BEATS EXPIRY DATES

Printed expiry dates are static estimates based on average conditions. They don't account for your actual storage temperature, humidity, or the specific batch of food you bought. MOS gas detection measures the actual biochemical state of food in real time — giving you ground truth instead of a guess.

Machine Learning

The AI Brain Behind the Sensing

Raw gas data isn't useful until it's interpreted. Our hybrid ML architecture turns ppm readings into actionable freshness intelligence.

🌳 Random Forest Classifier

An ensemble decision-tree model trained on thousands of labeled multi-gas samples from controlled spoilage experiments. It excels at pattern classification — recognizing that a specific combination of ethylene at 12 ppm + ammonia at 3 ppm means "Caution" for chicken breast, but "Fresh" for aged cheese.

OUTPUT

Multi-class classification → Fresh · Caution · Spoiled

🔄 LSTM Neural Network

A Long Short-Term Memory recurrent neural network that captures temporal patterns in gas concentration changes over time. While the Random Forest classifies the current state, the LSTM looks at how fast gases are changing — enabling it to predict hours remaining until spoilage.

OUTPUT

Temporal regression → Hours remaining until spoilage

Training Data Pipeline

Our models are trained on data collected from controlled spoilage experiments conducted across dozens of food types. Each experiment records gas concentration time series from the moment food is placed in a controlled fridge environment until it reaches full spoilage — labeled by food scientists at each stage. This dataset grows continuously as deployed units contribute anonymized readings.
